# Accessible Piece Playbook

**Use when creating, revising, or reviewing the Accessible Piece — the plain-language account of the project for a reader with no technical background, and the artifact Dandelion shares widely at the end of every project.**

**Required inputs:**
- The finished `Technical Report/` (the source of record for what was done and found).
- The `Claim Sheet.md` (for the honest success/failure framing).
- `Project Details/Project Details.md` — for Dandelion's mission and values, which this piece carries.
- Any progress reports already written (their language and teaching moments can be reused here).

**Output:**
- A standalone, engaging, honest piece such that a reader with no prior context comes away knowing what was done, why it mattered, and what the result was — including when the result is a clean negative — and *cares* about it.
- The canonical piece is authored in **Markdown** in the repo; a polished **PDF export** is produced as the shareable deliverable (the version posted to LinkedIn, Bluesky, and anywhere else the work meets the world).

**Applies these shared standards (defined in Project Details, not restated here):** the uncertainty/claim-discipline ethic (honest at true strength, negatives shown as results), Dandelion's identity and mission (the values this piece carries come from there), and credible-source linking for any concept the reader isn't expected to know.

---

## Purpose

The Accessible Piece is the project's front door for the general public — and, in practice, **the single artifact the director shares at the close of every project.** That gives it two jobs at once, and both are required:

1. **Inform, honestly.** A reader with no prior context finishes it understanding the real work and the real result. This is where lay-accessibility properly lives, so the Claim Sheet and Technical Report don't have to carry it.
2. **Make the reader care.** The piece should make someone who has never heard of Dandelion understand *why this question matters for everyday people* and feel the motivation behind the work. Dandelion's values — real research turned into affordable technology aimed at ordinary lives, honesty over hype, a clean "no" treated as a genuine contribution — are **woven through the piece**, not bolted on as a closing paragraph.

It is **not** a marketing summary of the Technical Report and not a press release. It is a separate piece of writing aimed at a different reader, and it earns its emotion from the work itself.

## Construction sequence

1. **Written at Phase 3** by the default writer (Claude writes, Codex reviews), from the finished Technical Report.
2. **Find the human throughline first.** Before drafting, name the question, why it matters to ordinary people, and the honest answer — and build the piece around that, not around the report's section order.
3. **Open on why it matters, not on method.** Lead with the stakes a non-specialist already feels — the everyday problem, the person it would help — so the reader cares before the science arrives.
4. **Weave Dandelion's motivation through, honestly.** Connect this specific project to the larger mission (affordable technology that helps everyday people; honest, public, careful work). Let the values be *felt* in how the work is told, not stated as a slogan — and never used to dress up a thin result.
5. **Explain every leaned-on concept.** Any term the reader isn't expected to know gets a simple explanation; link a credible source for those who want to go deeper.
6. **Keep it honest at true strength.** State the result for what it is. A clean negative is told as a real, valuable result — shared with the *same* conviction as a positive one, because the honesty is itself the brand. Never spun, buried, or inflated.
7. **Engaging is part of the bar, not a luxury.** It should be a genuine pleasure to read while remaining accurate. Reuse vivid framings and teaching moments from the progress reports where they fit.
8. **Produce the shareable PDF.** Export a clean, well-formatted PDF from the Markdown source. The Markdown file is the canonical, version-controlled artifact in the repo; the PDF is the object the director actually shares. Keep them in sync — regenerate the PDF whenever the piece changes.

## Quality checklist

- [ ] A reader with **no** prior context comes away knowing what was done, why it mattered, and what the result was.
- [ ] The piece makes the reader *care* — it opens on why the question matters and carries Dandelion's motivation throughout.
- [ ] Dandelion's values are woven in and **earned by the work**, not bolted on or used to inflate a thin result.
- [ ] No jargon walls; every leaned-on term explained, with a credible-source link where useful.
- [ ] The result is stated at true strength — negatives shown as results, shared with the same conviction as a positive, nothing overstated.
- [ ] It reads as its own piece, not a reskinned Technical Report.
- [ ] Engaging and clear throughout; honest throughout.
- [ ] A polished PDF export exists and matches the current Markdown source.

## Common failure modes

- **Marketing-summary drift.** Turning it into a highlights reel of the report. It's a separate piece for a different reader.
- **Values as veneer.** Pasting the mission on at the end, or using Dandelion's motivation to make a weak result feel bigger than it is. The values are carried by *how the real work is told*, and the work has to earn them.
- **Spin on a negative.** Dressing a clean "no" as a partial win. The honest negative is the asset; share it with conviction, protect it.
- **Jargon left standing.** Assuming the reader knows a term because the report did.
- **Dryness.** Accurate but unreadable. For this artifact, engaging is a requirement.
- **Overstatement.** Letting enthusiasm — or the wish for a more shareable win — push the claim past what the report supports.
- **PDF drift.** Sharing a PDF that no longer matches the updated Markdown source. Regenerate on every change.
