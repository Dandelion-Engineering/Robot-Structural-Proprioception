# Literature Foundation — Claude

**Project:** Robot Structural Proprioception
**Phase:** 0 (Literature Review) · **Author:** Claude · **Session 1, 2026-07-16**

*This is my independent Phase-0 reading of the field, written before the Claim Sheet so its method choices, baselines, metrics, and success/failure shapes are grounded in what the field has established. Every source was located through a real web search this session and its link/DOI verified; the full ledger-quality entries live in [`references.md`](references.md), which this document's Section 6 indexes. Codex is writing a separate, independent foundation — the divergence between our readings is intentional and preserved.*

**One-paragraph orientation.** The seed asks whether distributed, physically-grounded measurements of a robot's own structural state let it recognize and compensate for changes in its body *or its sensors*, and whether that beats a conventional sensor suite. After surveying six adjacent literatures, my central finding is that each of them owns one piece of this and none owns the whole: **self-modeling** revises a body model but senses it through joints or cameras; **adaptive/fault-tolerant control** compensates fast but from a predefined fault taxonomy or a single conflated latent; **online system identification** re-estimates dynamics but hits a proven identifiability wall from joint-space data alone; **structural health monitoring** reads dense strain/vibration but stops before the control loop; **soft-robot & tactile proprioception** uses distributed internal sensing but to estimate *shape/external contact*, not *health*; and **sensor-fault diagnosis** flags anomalies but cannot cleanly say whether the *sensor* or the *plant* moved. The unoccupied seam that unites them is **source attribution under a revisable self-model**: when the command→sensor→motion relationship shifts, was it the structure, an actuator, or the sensor watching them — and does distributed structural sensing supply the redundancy that answers this and yields a control advantage?

## 1. Domain and methods landscape

I read the field as six lineages that rarely cite each other. I describe each by its methods and trade-offs, then name the seam.

**(a) Data-driven robot self-modeling (the Lipson lineage).** A robot with no prior model infers one from the correlation between its actions and its sensed outcomes, then uses that internal model to plan and to notice when it no longer matches reality. The line runs from Bongard–Zykov–Lipson's continuous self-modeling (2006), through Kwiatkowski & Lipson's task-agnostic deep self-model (2019), to increasingly *visual* self-models — Chen et al.'s full-body query-driven occupancy field (2022), Hu–Chen–Lipson's egocentric single-camera dynamics model (2025), and Li et al.'s Neural Jacobian Fields (Nature 2025), which control diverse robots from one camera with no assumptions about materials or actuation. The defining trend is a migration *from proprioception toward external vision*: modern self-models are powerful and general but depend on cameras observing the robot — precisely the sensing the project wants to move *inside* the structure. Crucially, every method in this lineage detects change by comparing prediction to observation and then **trusts the sensors**, attributing all discrepancy to the body.

**(b) Neuroscience-inspired body-schema learning.** A parallel community frames the self-model as an adaptive, multimodal *body schema* (Hoffmann et al. 2010), emphasizing plasticity — tool incorporation, growth, recalibration. The most concrete recent system, GeMuCo (Kawaharazuka et al. 2024), learns a sensor–actuator correlational model online (including its own network structure), updates it continuously, and performs anomaly detection — the closest prior art to a *revisable, online, anomaly-aware* body model. But like the Lipson line it senses through actuation/joint signals, not distributed structural state.

**(c) Adaptive & fault-tolerant control (FTC), and damage recovery.** Three sub-families compensate for a changed body. *Classical FTC* (surveyed for manipulators by Quamar & Nasir 2024) detects, isolates, and reconfigures around faults — but typically against a *predefined* fault taxonomy (a specific actuator torque loss, a specific encoder fault). *Behavior-repertoire adaptation* (Cully et al. 2015, Nature) recovers a working gait in under two minutes after damage by searching a precomputed behavior map — explicitly *without* diagnosing the cause. *Learning-based adaptation* (Nagabandi et al. 2019; Kumar et al.'s RMA 2021) infers a latent that is *sufficient for control* from recent proprioceptive history and adapts in a fraction of a second. RMA is the paradigm competitor: it adapts to payload, terrain, and wear from joint encoders and an IMU alone. The trade-off across the three: predefined-taxonomy FTC is interpretable but brittle to unanticipated change; repertoire and latent methods are robust and fast but say nothing about *what* changed.

**(d) Online system identification & fast adaptation.** Where FTC compensates, sysID re-estimates. The estimation-theoretic line (recursive least squares; joint/dual Kalman estimation, tutorialized by Chebbi et al. 2025) recovers physical parameters online but requires *persistent excitation* and is bounded by a hard result: Wensing–Niemeyer–Slotine (2024) prove that certain inertial changes are "inertial transfers" that leave joint-space dynamics unchanged — **some structural changes are unlocalizable from joint torque + kinematics in principle.** The learning line (Local GP; Neural-Fly's fixed-basis linear adapter 2022; PEARL's probabilistic context 2019; CaDM 2020; meta-residual MPC 2025; CAPTURE's in-context sim-parameter inference 2024) trades interpretability for speed and robustness — but, again, compresses all variation into one latent. The estimation side *can* separate causes, but only under structural decoupling conditions or with added redundancy.

**(e) Structural health monitoring (SHM) & distributed strain sensing.** This is the field that already reads a body's internal mechanical state. SHM (Farrar & Worden 2007) is a statistical pattern-recognition pipeline over damage-sensitive features, organized by Rytter's levels — detection, localization, quantification, prognosis. Vibration/modal methods are global and cheap but blunt (natural-frequency shifts from damage are often sub-percent); guided-wave/EMI methods are exquisitely local but network-hungry; and the sensing modalities that could plausibly be embedded in a robot — fiber Bragg gratings, distributed fiber-optic (OFDR/Brillouin) strain, piezoelectrics — are mature and well-characterized (Barrias et al. 2016; Silveira et al. 2021). The decisive limitation for the project: SHM answers "is my *external* civil/aero structure damaged, and where?" and **explicitly stops before control.** Its axioms (Worden et al. 2007) don't concern acting on the estimate.

**(f) Soft-robot & tactile proprioception, and artificial skin.** This is the field that puts distributed sensing *inside* or *on* a robot. Soft-robot proprioception reconstructs continuous shape from embedded strain/curvature/magnetic/optical sensors, increasingly with learned estimators (Thuruthel et al. 2019; Baaij et al. 2023; Feliu-Talegon et al.'s 6D strain 2025). The single most relevant mechanism I found is Wang et al.'s (2024) "sensing expectation": the robot predicts the shape its actuation *should* produce and treats the prediction–measurement *residual* as external contact — a self-model residual doing double duty. Artificial skin / tactile sensing (GelSight, DIGIT, whole-body HEX-o-SKIN) is a mature ecosystem with its own simulators (TACTO, Taxim), but its semantics are almost entirely *external*: "what is touching me, where, how hard." Even fault-tolerant e-skins that survive being cut (Ozaki et al. 2025) preserve *external* readout, not structural self-knowledge.

**(g) Sensor-fault diagnosis (FDI/FDD).** Finally, the field that worries about the sensors themselves. Model-based FDI (Hwang et al. 2010; Patton & Chen 1994) generates residuals from observers/parity relations; Kalman/particle methods watch filter innovations (D'Amato et al. 2021 on IMUs); data-driven methods classify fault modes (bias, drift, stuck, dropout, scaling). Robotics-specific work targets encoder/IMU/F-T faults (Paviglianiti et al. 2010). The recurring wall — stated most cleanly by Sravani & Venkata (2023), who test detection *with and without* a real disturbance — is that a residual says "measurement disagrees with model" but not *why*: sensor fault, actuator fault, or genuine plant change produce overlapping signatures, and isolation needs structural decoupling or extra redundancy.

**The seam.** Lay these side by side and the gap is not subtle. Distributed structural sensing (e/f) is mature but points at damage-localization (no control) or shape/contact (no health). Self-model revision (a/b) is mature but senses through joints/vision and trusts its sensors. Fast adaptation (c/d) compensates but collapses cause into a latent, and is provably limited from joint-space data. FDI (g) worries about sensors but can't cleanly separate sensor from plant. **The project's thesis — distributed structural measurements as physically-grounded analytical redundancy that detects, localizes, and *attributes* a change (structure vs. actuator vs. sensor) with calibrated uncertainty, feeding a revisable self-model for adaptive control — sits in the intersection none of them occupy.**

## 2. Benchmark results (calibration for the Claim Sheet's success bars)

These are real numbers from the surveyed work; they set what "good" looks like and keep the Claim Sheet's bars grounded rather than invented.

**Self-modeling / adaptation performance (the baselines to beat):**
- Kwiatkowski & Lipson 2019: self-model learned from ~1,000 babbling trajectories in **<35 h**, consistent with the robot to **~4 cm**, **100%** closed-loop pick-and-place; damage detected and re-trained after a deformed part was installed.
- Chen et al. 2022: visual self-model accurate to **~1% of workspace**; supports damage detection *and localization*.
- Hu–Chen–Lipson 2025 (egocentric): next-state prediction error **1.9e-3 (visual) vs 3.1e-3 (action-only)**; recovered from a broken leg after **~7,000 babbling steps (~30 min)**; ran at ~5 Hz evaluating 100 candidate commands in parallel. *This action-vs-visual contrast is the template for the project's "advantage beyond a conventional suite" comparison.*
- Cully et al. 2015: compensatory gait in **<2 min** after damage, no self-diagnosis, from a ~13,000-behavior precomputed map.
- RMA (Kumar et al. 2021): 8-D latent inferred from **0.5 s** of history; sim success **73.5%** vs 76.2% (privileged), 62.4% (domain randomization), 56.5% (system-ID); real A1 carried ~100% of body weight.
- Neural-Fly 2022: adapts a 12-D coefficient vector at 50 Hz (**~3 ms** update), cutting tracking error **35–66%** vs strong baselines.

**Sensing resolutions & confounds (to make simulated signals physically credible):**
- FBG strain: **~1 µε** resolution, **1.2 pm/µε** sensitivity; temperature cross-sensitivity **12 pm/°C ≈ 10 µε/°C** — omitting thermal cross-sensitivity would be the least credible simplification.
- Distributed fiber (OFDR/Rayleigh): **~1 mm** spatial resolution over 50–70 m, ~1 µε; BOTDR ~1 m / 5 µε over tens of km. Galloway et al. 2019 in a soft actuator: strain every **0.8 mm**, up to **250 Hz**, sub-mm/sub-degree shape.
- Guided-wave: a **100 µm × 3 mm** notch detected in a 4.1 mm plate at 0.35–0.7 m.
- Environmental confound (Z24 bridge): seasonal natural-frequency swings of **several percent (first mode ~10%)** — *larger than* typical damage signatures. The simulation must inject competing environmental/thermal drift so self-recognition is realistically hard, not trivial.
- Soft-sensor drift: baseline RMSE **1.67–17.79°**, cut to **2.07–6.43°** only with continual-learning compensation — a direct handle on the drift-vs-structural-change confound.

**Fault-diagnosis performance (baselines for the detection/attribution task):**
- D'Amato et al. 2021 (IMU, particle filter): **99.5%** correct / **0.0%** wrong detection, **0.27 s** detection on abrupt bias; but **slow drift is the hardest mode** everywhere.
- Sravani & Venkata 2023: open/short faults <0.5 s; **drift 5.2–20 s**; adaptive **10–15%** thresholds needed to suppress false alarms under a real disturbance.
- Li et al. 2022: **98.9%** multi-mode sensor-fault classification in-distribution — but Hendriks et al. 2022 show the standard CWRU protocol leaks (same physical bearings train/test), and honest evaluation collapses reported accuracy to **~30–45%**. *Any learned detector here must be evaluated leakage-free.*

## 3. Dataset and resource landscape

**The simulation architecture that actually fits the hardware.** The project runs on one Windows 11 desktop (Ryzen 7 8700F, RTX 5060 Ti 16 GB, 32 GB RAM, Python 3.12). No single tool both runs a fast control/RL loop *and* emits credible distributed strain/vibration: fast RL engines model links as rigid (no intrinsic strain), while volumetric FEM is slow, stiff for metal-stiffness links, and doesn't fit a real-time loop. The realistic design is **hybrid** — a fast rigid-body engine supplies joint torques, contact forces, and body accelerations, which drive a **reduced-order structural model** (analytical Euler–Bernoulli/modal beam; PyElastica Cosserat rods for slender links; or a precomputed FEM reduced basis) to synthesize strain/curvature/vibration/temperature channels. Full FEM (SOFA/FEniCSx/Isaac deformable) is reserved for *offline* ground-truth generation and for validating the cheap model.

**Tooling (all commercial-use-friendly, licenses verified — full list with URLs in [`references.md`](references.md) §8):**
- Rigid-body / RL: **MuJoCo** (Apache-2.0, native-Windows, rich native sensor suite — strong primary candidate), **PyBullet** (zlib), **Isaac Lab/Sim** (BSD-3 / mixed; RTX 5060 Ti sits at the 16 GB VRAM floor), **Genesis** (Apache-2.0), **Brax/MJX** (Apache-2.0, GPU via WSL2 on this box).
- Structural / deformable: **PyElastica** (MIT, native-Windows, Cosserat strain/curvature/vibration on slender links — best-fit reduced-order model), **SOFA** (LGPL-2.1+), **FEniCSx** (LGPL-3+), **Warp** / **Taichi/DiffTaichi** (Apache-2.0, native-Windows GPU). Differentiable sim is attractive but immature at contact (Newbury et al. 2024) — not a foundation to depend on.
- Models/benchmarks: **MuJoCo Menagerie** (Apache-2.0), **dm_control** (Apache-2.0), **Gymnasium** (MIT).

**Datasets (there is a real gap here):**
- **LUMO** (CC-BY-3.0) — a real lattice tower with strain gauges + accelerometers + temperature + reversible damage states + a companion FE model. The best openly-licensed real distributed-structural dataset; a validation/calibration target and sensor-noise reference even though it is a civil structure, not a robot.
- **IASC-ASCE / LANL three-story / Z24** SHM benchmarks — open structural-response data under damage and (Z24) the temperature-vs-damage reference.
- **CWRU bearing** — the vibration-fault benchmark, but **no formal license** (commercial terms unclear) and a documented leakage caveat.
- **No openly-licensed dataset exists that is simultaneously robot-mounted, distributed-structural, and permissive** — which is a resource gap the project's simulation could itself begin to fill, and a reason the work is naturally simulation-first.

## 4. Failure modes (known dead ends to design around)

- **Trusting the sensors.** Every self-model in lineage (a) attributes prediction–observation discrepancy to the *body* and implicitly trusts the sensors. A project that must adapt to *sensor* change too cannot inherit that assumption — this is the single most relevant known limitation.
- **Latent conflation.** RMA/CaDM/PEARL/Neural-Fly compress structure, actuator, terrain, and (critically) sensor change into one code optimized for control sufficiency, not physical faithfulness. Excellent for robustness, useless for attribution. The project must *not* reduce to a single conflated latent if it wants to answer "what changed."
- **Identifiability nullspace.** Wensing et al. prove joint-space data leaves some structural changes unobservable. So a null result from a *conventional-suite* baseline is expected on exactly those changes — the experiment must include change types that lie in that nullspace, or the comparison is unfair to the hypothesis.
- **Persistent excitation vs. safety.** Parametric online ID needs informative motion to converge; the aggressive motions that excite the regressor are the ones a possibly-damaged robot should avoid. Experiments must respect this tension rather than assume rich excitation.
- **The sensor-vs-plant wall.** FDI residuals flag disagreement, not cause; near-collinear fault signatures (actuator vs. velocity-sensor) are poorly separable without structural decoupling or extra redundancy. The project's bet is that distributed structural channels *are* that redundancy — but it must be shown, not assumed.
- **Sensor pathologies are real physics.** Distributed strain/optical sensors drift, hysterese, and are temperature-cross-sensitive; a simulation that idealizes these away would prove nothing transferable. Drift especially must be modeled, because separating reversible sensor drift from irreversible structural change *is* the crux.
- **Benchmark leakage & EOV.** Data-driven detectors over-report under leaky splits (CWRU) and degrade under environmental variation. Evaluation must be leakage-free and must include environmental confounds at realistic (Z24-scale) magnitude.
- **Over-claiming morphological computation.** Müller & Hoffmann (2017) caution that the body rarely "computes." The value proposition is richer *information* from structural sensing, not the structure doing control — frame it that way.

## 5. Open questions (feeding Slot 3 — the transferable claim)

The literature leaves a specific, defensible gap. Stated as candidate claims, sharpest first:

1. **Source attribution.** No surveyed method emits a calibrated posterior over *which subsystem* — structure, actuator, or sensor — caused an observed change. Self-models trust sensors; fast-adaptation latents conflate causes; FDI gives hard decisions under decoupling assumptions; the most advanced uncertainty work (Zhou et al. 2021/2023) flags "unknown fault mode" but does not localize to a physical subsystem. **A structured latent that partitions structural/actuator/sensor causes, with a posterior over that partition, is essentially absent — and is the project's clearest possible contribution.**

2. **Distributed structural sensing as the disambiguating redundancy.** Wensing et al. prove joint-space data leaves an identifiability nullspace. The natural question: *what does a distributed strain/curvature/vibration field add to the identifiable subspace?* A formal or empirical observability analysis of this appears not to have been done, and it is exactly what would justify the extra sensing.

3. **The head-to-head advantage.** There is **no** benchmark contrasting a distributed-structural self-model against a conventional-suite self-model on the same adaptation task. The egocentric paper shows visual > action-only for prediction; Van Meerbeek shows structural sensing is accurate in isolation; but the marginal value of *distributed structural state over a conventional suite* — for (i) speed of change detection, (ii) accuracy of source attribution, and (iii) closed-loop tracking after a body/sensor change — is unmeasured. This comparison *is* the project's core experiment; the literature supplies the baselines (RMA, Cully, Paviglianiti) but not the answer.

4. **Repurposing the residual.** Wang et al.'s "sensing expectation" residual is the right *tool* pointed at the wrong *target* (external contact). Can a predicted-vs-measured *structural-state* residual be repurposed for fault/sensor-degradation detection and localization? This is a concrete, small, testable sub-question.

**Smallest sufficient research question (my proposal for Phase 1).** Reduce the seed to: *In a simulated articulated robot instrumented with a distributed structural-sensor field (strain/curvature/vibration, with credible resolution, drift, and thermal cross-sensitivity), does that field let an online estimator detect, localize, and attribute a controlled library of body changes (stiffness loss, added compliance/backlash, actuator weakening) and sensor faults (bias, drift, noise, dropout) — and does attribution-informed adaptation beat a conventional-suite baseline (encoders + IMU, RMA-style) on detection latency, attribution accuracy, and post-change tracking?* A clean negative — "distributed structural signals add little beyond the conventional suite, except on nullspace changes" — is itself a publishable, valuable result and should be a pre-declared success shape.
