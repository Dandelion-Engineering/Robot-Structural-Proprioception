# Dandelion Engineering — Research Project Details

## Introduction

### About Dandelion Engineering

Dandelion Engineering is a research and technology company with a single purpose: to do real research, and to turn what we learn from it into affordable technology that materially improves the lives of everyday people.

The name comes from Carl Sagan's *Cosmos*, where a dandelion seed serves as the ship of the imagination — small, durable, carried far by the wind, capable of taking root almost anywhere. That image captures what we are trying to be: not a large institution, not a heavily capitalized startup chasing scale, but something seed-shaped — light enough to travel, persistent enough to land somewhere it matters.

We are not building software for software companies. We are not building productivity tools for office workers. We are not optimizing existing workflows for incremental gains. The current generation of AI is the most significant technological shift in a generation, and most of what is being built with it is being aimed at problems that did not need solving. We want to aim it somewhere else.

Specifically: at science, at engineering, and at devices and services that individuals and families could use directly to improve daily life. The bar is whether what we build would, if it succeeded, actually make someone's life better. If the answer is no, it is not for us, regardless of how lucrative it might be.

Dandelion is not a venture-scale company. It is not built to maximize profit, and it is not run by anyone who wants to be a billionaire. It is built to be sustainable, to eventually fully financially support its founder and his family at a comfortable income level, and to spend the rest of its capacity on work that matters.

### About the Founder

Dandelion was founded by Randy Crespo. Randy spent seven years living with severe chronic pain that took him out of school and out of his life. What ended that period was a structured, brain-based approach to pain — learning, in real detail, how his own nervous system had learned to produce it, and how it could learn its way out again. That experience left him with two convictions that now shape everything he works on. First, that translating real science into something an ordinary person can actually use and benefit from is some of the most valuable work there is, whatever the field. Second, that the distance between what researchers already know and what actually reaches the people who need it is where most of the value is being lost — and where he wants to spend his effort.

Randy is not a specialist. He came close to finishing an aerospace engineering degree but decided that wasn't the right path for him. He is not credentialed in neuroscience, or in machine learning, or in engineering disciplines like robotics. He is a generalist — someone who reads broadly across subjects, generates ideas at the seams between them, and is genuinely happiest when he is learning something new. The structure of this project is built around that fact rather than against it. He directs; the agents execute. He brings the questions; the agents bring the technical depth required to pursue them. The collaboration is designed so that neither party has to do the other's job, and so that the work compounds across many ideas rather than getting bottlenecked on any one.

### The Long-Term Goal

The long-term goal is straightforward to state and difficult to reach: to do research that leads to affordable technology that actually helps everyday people.

Each word in that sentence carries weight. *Research* — meaning real investigation, real questions, real engagement with the literature, not retrofitted marketing. *Affordable* — meaning accessible to people who are not wealthy, not institutionally backed, not enterprise customers. *Actually helps* — meaning the test is whether someone's daily life improves, not whether a metric in a dashboard does. *Everyday people* — meaning the target is not a niche of professionals or a tier of paying users, but the kind of person you would meet anywhere, including the people in your own family.

What makes this possible now, and not a decade ago, is the combination of two things. The first is that AI systems have become genuinely capable of doing serious technical work — research synthesis, data analysis, software engineering, scientific writing — at a quality and speed that did not exist before. The second is that we have built a structured way for one human and several AI agents to work together over long stretches of time, with persistent memory and clear documentation, on problems that no one of them could complete alone. Together, those two things make it realistic for a small team — one person and a handful of AI collaborators — to produce work that would historically have required a much larger and much better-funded organization.

This is what Dandelion is, in operational terms: a small, deliberate, long-running collaboration between one human director and a team of AI agents, organized around the question of what happens if we point this technology at problems that matter for normal people. We are not racing anyone. We are not optimizing for growth. We are trying to do honest, careful work in public, one project at a time, and to build something durable from the result.

If you are an agent reading this at the start of a new project: you are part of that team. The work in front of you is not throwaway. It is one piece of a longer effort, and the standards are the standards of someone who actually wants the result to be useful. Treat it accordingly.

## Strategy: Time

Most companies are organized around urgency. They have investors who need returns on a schedule, employees whose salaries are due at the end of the month, and competitors whose progress punishes slowness. The result is that almost every company is, structurally, in a hurry — and the work it produces reflects that. Corners get cut. Decisions get made before they are ready. Projects that would have been excellent if given another year ship as merely good, because shipping is what survival requires.

Dandelion is not organized that way. It does not have investors. It is not racing competitors. Its founder got a job as a final inspector at an aerospace manufacturing company in order to have a steady income that at least covers his bills, which means the company itself does not need to produce revenue on any particular timeline. That single fact reorganizes everything downstream of it.

Our strategy, stated plainly, is time. Not money — we do not have much of it currently. Not headcount — we are one human and a small team of AI agents. Not speed — we are not optimizing for it. The thing we have that other organizations our size do not is the freedom to let a project grow at its own natural rate, for as long as it takes, without anyone needing it to be done.

This is possible because of how the team is built. Each session of work is a small unit. An agent reads the project's history, picks up where the previous session left off, contributes something — a piece of research, a draft, a piece of code, a question — and writes a clear note for the next session. None of that work is lost. The persistent memory of the project means an hour of careful effort today connects directly to an hour of careful effort tomorrow, and the day after that, and the day after that. Sessions stack. They do not have to be heroic. They have to be honest.

Over many sessions, that stacking becomes substantial. A project that no individual person could justify spending six straight months on can absolutely be completed across six months of short sessions, with the human checking in periodically and the agents doing the cumulative work in between. The unit is small; the integral is large. This is the inversion that AI collaboration makes available.

The "little by little" posture also frees Dandelion from the failure mode of needing every project to be the right one. When projects grow at their natural pace and compound across many of them, no single project has to carry the company. As resources allow, multiple Collaboration Stations run in parallel, each one exploring a different idea at its own pace. Some ideas will turn out to matter; some will turn out to be smaller than they looked; some will surprise everyone. The portfolio is the strategy. Time is what makes a portfolio possible without venture funding.

There is a discipline to this, and it is worth stating clearly: patience without structure is just delay. Each Collaboration Station has a defined scope — a specific idea, sharpened into an executable brief before serious work begins, with a clear definition of what completion looks like for that particular idea. Projects do not run forever; they run until they have reached the stopping point that was identified for them. What changes is not whether a project ends, but when. The answer is: when the work is ready, not when the calendar says so.

The dandelion image carries the strategy as well as it carried the company. Seeds do not arrive on a schedule. They land where the wind takes them, and they take their time once they do. They are also patient enough, and durable enough, to land in places nothing else would think to grow.

If you are an agent working inside a Dandelion Collaboration Station: your session does not have to finish anything. It has to contribute something real, document it clearly, and hand it cleanly to whatever comes next. The compounding is what does the work. You are part of how that compounding happens.

## Structure: The Project Form

Time as a strategy only works if the work itself has a shape. A project that grows little by little, across many short sessions, run by collaborators who rotate in and out of context, has to be held together by something more durable than memory or momentum. Without that, "no rush" turns into "no end." With it, every session has a clear place to plug into, every contribution lands in a defined slot, and the project moves forward whether or not any single person is paying attention to it on a given day.

The thing that holds a Dandelion project together is the **Claim Sheet**. Every project begins with one. The Claim Sheet is a single document, drafted collaboratively by the agents during Phase 1, and it is the contract for the project — the shared reference that every agent reads at the start of every session, and that every result is measured against.

### The Claim Sheet and its companion

The Claim Sheet is general enough to fit any project Dandelion takes on, regardless of subject. It is organized as **fifteen numbered slots**, each a structurally important commitment: the domain and substrate; the problem; the transferable claim; constraints; methods and baselines; application and downstream relevance; the evaluation design; the director's verification path; the architecture or build plan; the computational and physical environment; the pre-declared shapes of success, failure, and inconclusive results; the minimum public artifact required to conclude; and the possible monetization paths. Not every slot is heavy for every project, but all are present so nothing structurally important is left implicit, and so an outside reader can find any of these answers without reconstructing them.

The Claim Sheet leans technical by necessity — its most frequent readers are the agents executing the work. So every project also produces, at the close of Phase 1, an **Accessible Claim Sheet**: the same content translated into plain language, the director's reference companion to the contract. The technical sheet remains the contract for the team; the accessible sheet is how the director holds it. Both are required, and they are kept in sync.

How to build, fill, and review each — including the readability requirements that keep the technical sheet legible without softening its rigor — lives in the playbooks (see *The Playbooks*, below): `Playbooks/claim-sheet.md` and `Playbooks/accessible-claim-sheet.md`.

### The amendment protocol — keeping the contract honest

Real research surfaces things the Claim Sheet did not anticipate. A method does not work the way the literature said it would. A signal turns out to be more interesting than the one we set out to study. A side question opens up that is, on inspection, more important than the original. This is not a failure of planning; it is what investigation does.

The structure allows this without losing its grip. When something unexpected appears, the agent who finds it writes a short **amendment proposal** — what was found, why it changes the path, the new path, and the new success, failure, and non-transfer shapes. The other agents approve or reject before execution shifts. An approved amendment is **appended and dated, never overwritten** on the original; if it invalidates work already done, the affected files are moved to a dated `archive/` subfolder rather than deleted, so the trail stays visible. Drift becomes a recorded turn rather than a quiet erosion.

This same protocol covers the project's entry point and its labor split. When the director reviews the Claim Sheet, that review is the first invocation of the amendment protocol; when the division of labor needs to change, the change runs through it too. One protocol, applied at every scale where the plan can move. The full mechanics — including how the director's review and approval flow as **non-blocking** — are in `Playbooks/claim-sheet.md`.

### What every project leaves behind

A Dandelion project is not finished when the work is done. It is finished when the work is *shippable* — when it has been turned into artifacts that can leave the building and stand on their own. Every project produces **three outward-facing artifacts and one director-facing artifact**:

1. **A Technical Report** — rigorous and complete, written in LaTeX for scientists or engineers in the relevant field. Its required contents come from the Claim Sheet's "minimum public artifact" slot. → `Playbooks/technical-report.md`
2. **An Accessible Piece** — the same project written for a reader with no technical background: engaging, honest, free of jargon walls. This is also **the artifact the director shares publicly at the close of every project** (LinkedIn, Bluesky, etc.), so it must both inform honestly and make the reader care, with Dandelion's values earned by the work rather than bolted on. → `Playbooks/accessible-piece.md`
3. **A Reproducibility Packet** — code, configurations, data references, a top-level runbook README, a `DATA.md` for dataset access, and the **Slot 8 verification artifact**, organized so an outside person can copy the packet folder alone to a clean machine and reproduce the result without contacting anyone on the team. → `Playbooks/reproducibility-packet.md`
4. **A Study Guide** (director-facing) — written for Randy specifically, in **two passes**: Pass 1 (Conceptual Foundation) at Phase 1 close, and Pass 2 (Concept Delta) at Phase 3, the latter under a strict no-spoiler rule. Its job is to keep the director able to follow and judge the work himself. → `Playbooks/study-guide.md`

All four are required for a project to be considered complete. The three outward-facing artifacts are how the work becomes a public contribution; the Study Guide is how the director stays able to follow and evaluate it.

When a project is **released publicly**, it also carries a **Live-Run README** at the repository root — the first thing a stranger sees. While the project runs it shows a status banner and a lean running log; when the project concludes it resolves into an honest landing page that leads with the result and a way to verify it. → `Playbooks/live-run-readme.md`

**Default writer convention.** Claude is the default writer for the Claim Sheet, the Accessible Claim Sheet, the Technical Report, the Accessible Piece, and both Study Guide passes; Codex is the required reviewer and gives final approval on each. Both agents contribute to the Reproducibility Packet, since its contents come out of both agents' execution, and they reconcile their references files into the Technical Report's bibliography at Phase 2. This is the default starting point for the labor split at Phase 1; like the rest of the split, it can be amended through the standard protocol if a project calls for it.

### The Playbooks — how each artifact is built

This document is the project's constitution: who Dandelion is, the strategy, the working method, and the standards every artifact must meet. The step-by-step procedure for *building or reviewing* each artifact does **not** live here — it lives in a dedicated **Playbook**, one markdown file per artifact, in the `Playbooks/` folder at the project root. An agent reads a playbook only when it is actually building or reviewing that artifact, which keeps this document lean and keeps each session from carrying construction instructions it does not currently need.

A few conventions:

- **"Playbook," not "skill."** This is a custom Dandelion convention, not the official agent "skills" tool. We reserve the word *skill* for the tool and call each of these files a *playbook*, so there is no confusion and no dependence on either agent's auto-discovery — both agents simply read the file the routing table points to.
- **Anti-drift rule.** A playbook carries only what is specific to building its artifact (purpose, inputs, construction sequence, quality checklist, failure modes, a compact example). It **references** the shared Standards below — it does not restate them. There is one source of truth per standard, and it is the Standards section of this document.
- **The routing table is the index.** The table below is what an agent consults to decide which playbook to open and when. It is the always-loaded layer; the playbooks themselves are loaded on demand.

### The shape, in one line

The Claim Sheet binds the work; the amendment protocol keeps the binding honest to what the project finds; the three outward-facing deliverables are how the work leaves the building; the two-pass Study Guide is how the director stays able to follow and judge it; and the playbooks are how each of those is built to the same standard every time.

If you are an agent working inside a Dandelion Collaboration Station: the structure is here so the work can compound across sessions, across collaborators, and across years. Read the Claim Sheet. Make your contribution land in one of its slots. When you build or review an artifact, open its playbook first. If you find something that changes what the sheet should say, propose the amendment rather than working around it. The form is the thing that lets the work be slow and still get somewhere.

## Working Method

Strategy is time and structure is the Claim Sheet, but neither of those alone tells the team how a session actually runs. Agents arrive with no memory of the previous day's work, several of them are working in parallel, and the human director is not always in the loop. Without some shared sense of how the work moves, even a well-formed project can stall — not because anyone is doing the wrong thing, but because no one is sure whose turn it is, what the current state is, or whether they should be reviewing someone else's work or pushing forward on their own. This section names the few rules that keep the work moving without overspecifying it.

The principle behind those rules is the same one that runs through the rest of this document: as little instruction as the work can tolerate, and no less. Every additional rule is context that has to be loaded at the start of every session, and context that crowds out the actual thinking. The defaults from `AgentPrompt.md` already cover most of the operational surface — session reports, chat updates, the standard workflow. What follows is what specifically a Dandelion project adds on top of that.

### Phases

A Dandelion project has four coarse phases. They are not a strict pipeline; later phases can begin before earlier ones are fully closed, and the project can loop back through the amendment protocol when needed. But naming the phases gives every session a way to know roughly where the project is.

1. **Phase 0 — Literature Review.** Before the Claim Sheet is drafted, each agent independently surveys the field the project idea sits in, so that the Claim Sheet's method choices, baselines, metrics, and success/failure shapes are informed by what the field has established rather than invented in the same session the contract is written. Each agent produces a **Literature Foundation** document in their personal workspace, built from real web searches with verifiable links or DOIs (no citing from memory). Phase 0 closes when both documents are written and the agents have compared notes in chat; the director is not involved. How to build it → `Playbooks/literature-foundation.md`.
2. **Phase 1 — Sharpening.** The agents collaboratively build the Claim Sheet and then the Accessible Claim Sheet, drawing on the Phase 0 Literature Foundations, and produce **Pass 1 of the Study Guide**. Phase 1 closes when the agents agree on those documents and decide the initial division of labor; at that point they log a *Claim Sheet ready for director review* entry in `director_requests.md` and Phase 2 begins. The director's review of the Claim Sheet, whenever it lands, is treated as the first amendment cycle (see *The amendment protocol*) and is **non-blocking** — the agents keep working while it is pending.
3. **Phase 2 — Execution.** The work happens: research, building, analysis, drafting. Most sessions live here. Amendments to the Claim Sheet, when the work calls for them, also happen in this phase.
4. **Phase 3 — Deliverables.** The three outward-facing artifacts — Technical Report, Accessible Piece, Reproducibility Packet — plus **Pass 2 of the Study Guide** get produced and finalized. The project is not complete until all four exist, meet the bar set by the Claim Sheet, and are explicitly approved by all agents. Once they are, Phase 3 closes and the project is finished as scoped.

A project's completion is its natural endpoint, not a transition to a new phase. Once Phase 3 closes, no new work should be added unless the director gives an explicit signal to continue — a new request, an amendment proposal, or an expanded scope. If the director has set up automation that continues to launch agent sessions past that endpoint, an agent that realizes it has been initialized into a completed project should end the session without adding new work, rather than inventing follow-on tasks. That includes not writing a human report or updating the summary of only necessary context. It should be like the session never happened. The framework's strategy is to let projects end where they were designed to end.

A session belongs to whichever phase its work belongs to, not to whichever phase the project as a whole is in. An agent drafting the Accessible Piece while the technical work is still finishing is doing Phase 3 work in a Phase 2 project, and that is fine. The phases describe the work being done, not a gate the project has to pass through in order.

### Division of labor

The initial division of labor is decided by the agents themselves inside Phase 1, after the Claim Sheet is drafted. They write the proposal in a chat folder where all of them can see it, discuss until they agree, and begin execution once they have agreed. The human director is not part of this conversation — the agents know their own capacities and the shape of the work better than the director does at this stage, and giving them the call keeps the director out of a decision that does not need them. The bar for the proposal is simple: every Claim Sheet slot has someone responsible for the work that produces or addresses it, and every agent knows what they are doing first.

The division is not fixed. Across the life of a project, work pauses, gets abandoned, splits, and appears. When that happens, the agent who notices proposes an amendment — to the Claim Sheet, and to the labor proposal when the labor split is implicated — using the same amendment protocol the Structure section defines. One protocol, two scales: the project's plan and the labor that executes it.

### When the director is the dependency

Some work can only be done by the director — downloading a dataset behind a login, signing up for an API, making a call that requires his identity or judgment — and the director is not always available. The project cannot stall every time this gap appears, and the agents cannot guess at what the director would have wanted. The discipline that handles this is a single, persistent, append-only file at the project root: **`director_requests.md`**. When an agent hits director-only work, it appends an entry naming what is needed, why, what is blocked on it, and — when one exists — a fallback the agents can take meanwhile; then it **keeps moving** on other Phase-2 work or on Phase-3 work that can start early. The director replies in place when he resolves a request, and the original entry stays as a record. This file is the single source of truth for what the director is on the hook for. The full protocol → `Playbooks/director-requests.md`.

### Cross-review

The agents are working in parallel on related pieces of a single project. If they do not read each other's work, the project ends up as several disconnected efforts that happen to share a folder. The rule is light: each agent must read at least one other agent's recent work each session and respond to it if it is called for. *Recent* means the most recent human report is read, the relevant work that report points to is read, and any relevant active chats the other agent is part of are read. *Respond* means substantively if there is something to say — a question, a correction, a connection to one's own work that hasn't been discussed. The agent chooses what to read and when in the session to do it. The bar is that it happens, not that it happens at a specific moment. **It is possible for one agent to still be on the same session while another has already done several; only the most recent unreviewed human report needs to be read, since older ones are subsumed by it and by the chats and working files the reviewer encounters anyway.**

The discipline that makes this work without spiraling is that **corrections propagate forward, not backward**. If an agent finds a problem in another agent's earlier work, the fix lives in current and future work — the next draft, the next analysis, the next deliverable — not in reopening the earlier piece for revision. The earlier piece is a recorded turn; what comes after it is where the correction lives. This is the same shape as the amendment protocol — append, never overwrite — and it is what keeps cross-review from collapsing into two agents re-editing the same paragraph across many sessions while the project itself does not move.

Pushback is allowed and expected. If an agent disagrees with another's feedback, they say so in chat and the disagreement gets resolved before either moves on. The default is forward motion; the exception is a real disagreement that needs settling before the project can keep going. Disagreements left unsettled tend to come back later, larger, with more sunk into them. Settle them when they arrive.

Every artifact being reviewed while it is still active runs through an explicit **review cycle**. The owning agent hands off a state it explicitly approves; the reviewing agent reviews and may edit the artifact directly, then hands it back in the chat stating what changed and **explicitly approving that state**; the owning agent must then re-open the artifact, genuinely re-review both the feedback and the edits, and either explicitly approve or edit and hand back. The loop closes only when both agents have explicitly approved the *same* state — approval is never inferred from creation, an edit, a handoff, or silence. This depends on the owning agent actually **returning to the artifact after it is reviewed**; if it never does, the re-review never happens and the "agreed" state is a fiction. Read `Playbooks/review-cycle.md` before reviewing an artifact or responding to a review of your own, and escalate a disagreement that hasn't converged in about two round-trips to the director rather than looping. This is distinct from the forward-propagation rule above, which governs problems found in already-concluded work; the review cycle never reopens concluded artifacts.

**The review cycle does not apply to the general recent work review (where an agent reviews the most recent human report and the work it points to) until an artifact is flagged by that general recent work review. This is to avoid having to explicitly approve the general recent work review when no problems are found.**

### Live-run status (public runs only)

If the project has been released publicly, it carries a Live-Run README at its root. At the end of each session's work — before you create your end of session documents/edits (human report, summary of only necessary context update) — check whether the session finished an artifact, closed a phase, or produced something genuinely noteworthy. If so, update the README's banner and running log per `Playbooks/live-run-readme.md`. If not, leave it untouched; the log is lean by design. This is a heartbeat check, not a per-session journal entry: the point is that the check happens every session, not that the README changes every session.

### What this section is, and what it isn't

Five rules: phases that name where the project is, a labor split the agents own and amend through the existing protocol, a protocol for the moments only the director can resolve, a cross-review discipline that keeps the work connected without trapping it in revision loops — with an explicit review cycle for artifacts still in active review — and a public-run heartbeat that keeps the Live-Run README current. That is the whole working method a Dandelion project adds on top of the defaults in `AgentPrompt.md`. Anything else — when in the session to write the report, how often to check chats, what tone to use in handoffs — is left to the agents, because the defaults already cover it and because every additional rule is context that has to be carried.

If you are an agent working inside a Dandelion Collaboration Station: know what phase your session's work belongs to, know what part of the labor split you own, log any director-only blockers in `director_requests.md` and keep moving on what is not blocked, and read at least your collaborators' recent contributions before you finish. If the project is public, check at the end of the session whether anything this session is worth reflecting in the Live-Run README. If something needs to change, propose the amendment. If you disagree with feedback, say so. The work moves forward; the record holds the trail.

## Keeping the Director in the Loop

The director approves the Claim Sheet through the amendment protocol, and the agents own the project from there. That setup only works if the director can stay close enough to the work to know whether the result, when it arrives, is real. Too close and the director becomes a bottleneck the rest of the document was built to remove; too far and the project lands in front of someone who has lost the thread and cannot evaluate what they are looking at. Three mechanisms hold that distance correctly — the **progress report**, which keeps the director current on where the work is; the **director's verification path**, which gives the director a way to check the result without doing the technical work; and the **Study Guide** (specified in Structure and built per `Playbooks/study-guide.md`), which gives the director the conceptual equipment to follow and judge the work himself. The three are complementary: the progress report conveys the *current state*, the verification path offers *hands-on confirmation*, and the Study Guide builds the *conceptual understanding* that makes both of the others meaningful.

All three share one discipline: present the work so that someone who is not a domain expert can follow what is happening, why it matters, and what was found. They are not technical reports written for the team, and they are not marketing pieces written to impress. Each is a careful, honest translation of the real state of the work into something a generalist can engage with.

### The progress report

Every eighth session each agent runs — at that agent's own sessions 8, 16, 24, and so on (the count is per-agent, not cumulative across agents) — the agent writes a progress report in addition to its normal session work. Two further events trigger a report regardless of count: a **phase transition** and an **approved amendment to the Claim Sheet**, written by whichever agent's session closes the transition or writes the approving turn. These triggers stack and do not reset the per-agent counter. Reports live in a `Progress Reports/` folder in each agent's personal workspace, separate from the per-session human reports, and are written for the director at the bar of the Accessible Piece — clear, honest, jargon-free, with a credible-source link for any concept the reader is not expected to know. How to write one → `Playbooks/research-progress-report.md`.

### The director's verification path

The Claim Sheet's Slot 8 is the agents' commitment, made before execution begins, to build a specific artifact whose purpose is to let the director — and through them, anyone who is not a domain expert — verify the result without reading the Technical Report end to end. Its form depends on the project: a simulation, a side-by-side visualization against a baseline, a worked example from raw input through final output, an interactive demo. Whatever the form, it has two properties: it is hands-on enough that the director can engage with it directly rather than read a claim about it, and it is self-contained enough that engaging with it does not require technical context he does not have. Naming verification before execution begins also forces the agents to design the work so that verification is possible — a quiet discipline of its own.

The verification artifact lives **inside the Reproducibility Packet** rather than being a separate fourth deliverable, so anyone who downloads the packet can verify the work the same way the director does. It is paced into the project rather than assembled in the final session; if results change what it should be, the Slot 8 entry is amended through the normal protocol. The construction detail lives in `Playbooks/claim-sheet.md` (Slot 8) and `Playbooks/reproducibility-packet.md`.

### What this section is, and what it isn't

Three mechanisms — a recurring progress report at the Accessible-Piece bar, a verification artifact named at Phase 1 and housed in the Reproducibility Packet, and a two-pass Study Guide at the phase gates — hold the right distance between the director and the project: close enough to know the work is real, far enough not to be its bottleneck.

If you are an agent working inside a Dandelion Collaboration Station: every eighth session you run, complete normal session work first and then write the progress report — it is an addition to the session, not a replacement. If your session closes a phase transition or an approved amendment, write a report at that point too, and the per-agent count keeps advancing underneath the event. When you work on the verification artifact, build it at a pace that fits the project, and write it so the director can use it without you in the room. The Accessible Piece is not the only place the project meets its non-specialist reader; it is the last one.

## Standards

Strategy, structure, working method, and the director's loop describe how a Dandelion project moves. They do not describe the bar the work has to meet while it moves. This section does. **These are hard requirements, not preferences.** They apply to every Dandelion project, and to every session. Where a specific project needs to relax one of them, the relaxation is named in the Claim Sheet — written down, justified, and visible. Otherwise, the defaults below hold.

The point of naming them in one place is not to lecture. It is so that no agent has to guess what counts as good work inside a Dandelion project, and so that a stranger reading the artifacts later can tell that the work was done seriously. The playbooks reference these standards by name; this section is where they are defined.

### Scientific work

The work is investigation, not advocacy. Results are characterizations of what the data, signal, or system actually shows — not interpretations stretched past what the evidence supports. If a project has a validation step that is supposed to confirm the data or method behaves as expected (a known control, a published baseline, a sanity check), that step is a stop-or-go gate. If the expected pattern is not present, the team diagnoses before proceeding; it does not get papered over and pushed downstream.

Quality control is documented in the artifacts the project produces. Files excluded from the analysis, samples that failed a check, runs that were discarded — every exclusion is named, with the reason, somewhere a reader can find it. The Technical Report names them; the Reproducibility Packet preserves them. Silent exclusions are a reproducibility failure.

### Software engineering

Every script in a Dandelion project has one clear purpose. No monolithic notebooks where everything happens in one place. Shared logic — data loading, feature computation, plotting — lives in a `utils/` module and is imported, not copy-pasted across scripts.

Every script accepts its inputs via command-line arguments using `argparse`. No hard-coded paths. No bare globals that have to be edited to run the script on a different machine. Every function carries a docstring stating its inputs, outputs, and purpose. Scripts print their progress to stdout, write results to named output files, and fail loudly with informative errors when their inputs are wrong. Silent failure is the worst-case mode and the easiest one to fall into; the standards above are mostly there to prevent it.

Figures are produced at 300 DPI or higher, with labeled axes, titles, and consistent color and styling conventions across the project. A figure should be interpretable without the reader having to open the code that produced it.

Dependencies are pinned. `requirements.txt` includes version numbers. A reader six months from now should be able to install the same versions the project ran on and reproduce the results without hunting for the right release of a library.

### Reproducibility and portability

The Reproducibility Packet exists so that an outside reader can run the project on their own machine without contacting anyone on the team. That standard is impossible to meet if the project encodes the team's machine into its scripts. So:

- **No hard-coded paths in any script.** Machine-specific paths (data directory, output directory, model directory) are passed via CLI arguments with `required=True`. A script that silently runs against the wrong path is a reproducibility failure.
- **Outputs default to project-relative locations.** Scripts write to paths relative to the project root, never to absolute paths.
- **The reproducibility packet must run end-to-end on a fresh environment.** Given the correct data path and a fresh install from `requirements.txt`, every script must execute correctly. Validate this before declaring the packet complete.
- **The virtual environment is used as the director specifies.** The director provides a virtual environment for each project; the director names it and gives the exact invocation. Agents use that invocation for every Python and pip command. Never bare `python`, never bare `pip` — both pick up whatever interpreter the shell happens to have, and that interpreter is not the one the project's dependencies live in.

**Keep the packet's materials packet-ready as you go — and "packet-ready" means *inside the packet folder*, not merely portable wherever they sit.** Create `Reproducibility Packet/` early in Phase 2 and write surviving scripts directly into it as they are finalized (per the Software engineering standard above: portable, no hard-coded paths, argparse). Dependencies are pinned the moment they are installed; every excluded file, sample, or run is recorded the moment it is excluded. Phase-3 assembly is then curation and validation — trimming, writing the runbook and `DATA.md`, running the fresh-environment check on a **copy of the packet folder alone** — not relocating code that was written outside it. Referencing project-root scripts by relative path from the packet's own README is not self-contained, however portable those scripts are; the packet folder itself must hold everything its README tells a reader to run.

### Efficiency

Dandelion exists to build affordable technology. Affordability is not a marketing claim; it is a design constraint that begins at the first session of the first project and shapes every technical choice that follows. The discipline is: minimize cost — computational, financial, energetic, time — while still meeting the success bar named in the Claim Sheet.

A working solution is good. The smallest sufficient working solution is better. A model that runs is good; a model that runs on the same hardware in a quarter of the memory, while meeting the same success bar, is the one that ships. A pipeline that processes the data is good; a pipeline that does it in a tenth of the compute is the one that ends up being usable by the people we are building for. Every dollar of compute the project does not need, every gigabyte of memory it does not require, every minute of runtime it can save, is one more user who can use what we build on hardware they already own. Efficiency is the mechanism by which our mission survives contact with reality.

This is not a directive to over-optimize, and it is **not** a cap on the search. "Smallest sufficient" governs the *final shipped solution*, not the investigation that finds it: a null result from an undersized model is evidence about that model, not proof the signal is absent. The discipline is to explore enough capacity to actually settle the question — scaling up the hardware allows when there is partial signal or when a larger model could plausibly capture one a smaller one cannot — and then to ship the smallest version that meets the bar. Premature optimization on the wrong axis costs more than it saves; optimize where size, runtime, or cost determines whether the work can reach the people it is meant to reach, and leave the rest alone. The Claim Sheet's Slot 9 capacity ladder is where this is made concrete for a given project.

### Open source and licensing

The standard Dandelion project has no financial budget. Therefore, agents must only use the compute environment available to them and any open-source tools, libraries, datasets, etc. that are available for free.

Every library, dataset, pre-trained model, and tool used in a Dandelion project carries a documented license, and **commercial-use-permitting licenses are preferred by default** — permissive open source (MIT, BSD, Apache 2.0), copyleft licenses whose terms the project can honor (LGPL with dynamic linking, certain weak-copyleft variants), and openly published datasets with clear commercial-use clauses. Where a resource the project genuinely needs carries a more restrictive license, it may be used only under an **explicitly approved and named exception** that states the downstream limits the exception creates. Resources whose licensing is unclear are treated as unusable until the question is resolved; do not import on the assumption that it will be fine.

The same standard applies to what Dandelion produces and releases: the artifacts the project ships carry licenses that fit Dandelion's intent of doing public, reusable work, with the specific license named per project (and any non-default choice justified the same way). The Reproducibility Packet documents the licenses so a reader can audit every dependency without re-reading every dependency's repository; dataset licenses live in the packet's `DATA.md`.

### The references file

Every agent maintains a running `references.md` in its personal workspace for the project. Every paper, article, dataset documentation, technical blog, or other source that informed the agent's work gets an entry containing: a short summary of what the source covers and why it matters; a short note on how it informed the project — what decision it shaped, what method it justified, what question it answered or opened; a direct link where one exists; and a proper citation in a format that can be transferred into the Technical Report's bibliography without rewriting.

Each agent maintains its own file. Two agents reading the same paper from different angles produce different entries, and the divergence is information worth keeping. At Phase 2, the agents reconcile their files into the Technical Report's bibliography. The references files persist after the project is concluded. Maintaining `references.md` is part of the work, not an after-the-fact task: the right moment to write the entry is when the source is fresh and the reason for citing it is clear — not at the end of the project when memory has dimmed and citations get fabricated to fill the page.

### What this section is, and what it isn't

Six standards: rigor in the scientific work, discipline in the software, reproducibility and portability built in from the start, efficiency as a design constraint anchored to the mission, licenses that allow what we are trying to do, and a running references file that turns into the project's bibliography. None of them are aspirational. They are the bar, and the playbooks apply them artifact by artifact.

If you are an agent working inside a Dandelion Collaboration Station: write the script with `argparse` from the first version, not as a refactor at the end. Pin the dependency the moment you install it. Add the citation the moment you read the paper. Pick the smaller-and-sufficient option once both options actually meet the bar. Treat the standards as part of how the work gets done, not as a polish pass after the work is finished. The artifact that ships is the one that was built to these from the start.

## The Idea

This is the seed of the project — the raw idea the director has put forward as the thing this Collaboration Station exists to investigate. It is intentionally short and intentionally informal. *Project idea (to be filled in by the director):*


*Authorship note: The underlying observations, questions, and research motivation in this seed came from Randy Crespo. This project idea was written by ChatGPT Pro after Randy described those thoughts and developed them through a discussion with ChatGPT. ChatGPT organized the ideas, incorporated refinements that emerged during the discussion, and shaped them into this research seed and wrote it as if presented by Randy.*


I started with two related concerns about the direction of general-purpose humanoid robotics.

The first is mechanical. In many humanoid robots, powerful motors and transmissions are concentrated at or near the joints, and the controller must produce smooth, precise, contact-sensitive motion through a body that may be mechanically rigid, highly geared, or difficult to backdrive. The location of a motor at a joint may not be the fundamental problem by itself; the more important issue may be the stiffness, reflected inertia, friction, and fixed mechanical behavior introduced by the complete actuation and transmission system. Human bodies work differently. Muscles apply forces through tendons, often across joints, and the body can vary its apparent stiffness while also using elasticity and deformation as part of movement. This makes me wonder whether many robots are asking software to compensate for a body whose natural mechanics do not help it interact smoothly with the physical world.

The second concern is about how general robots are being trained. Scaling up collections of videos showing humans or robots performing different tasks may teach a system a great deal about objects, visible motion, and the sequence of an activity, but video leaves out much of the physical experience that makes an action work. It usually does not reveal the force being applied, whether contact is beginning to slip, how pressure is distributed, how an object is deforming, how much effort an actuator is producing, or how loads are moving through the body. Two actions can look almost identical in a video while involving very different physical states.

Human skin helped me see what may be missing. We can detect and localize contact across most of the body, and our nervous system combines that information with signals from muscles, tendons, joints, and balance organs. Robots have nothing comparable to this dense, distributed stream of bodily information. A robot may have cameras, joint encoders, and a few force sensors, but that is not the same as continuously sensing both its interaction with the environment and the changing mechanical condition of its own body.

Thinking about this made aerospace structures feel directly relevant to robotics. Stress, strain, shear, torsion, bending, vibration, and load paths are not only quantities used to determine whether a structure will survive. They may also be information a robot could use to understand itself. Sensors embedded in or distributed across a robot’s structure could measure physically meaningful deformation-related signals such as strain, curvature, shear, torsional deformation, vibration, or temperature. From those signals, an intelligent system might infer where loads are traveling, how its body is bending, whether its effective stiffness has changed, and whether the body is still responding in the way its internal model predicts. Surface “skin” could describe external contact, while structural sensing could provide a kind of internal proprioception.

That leads to the central idea for this project: a capable robot should not assume that its body is a permanent, perfectly calibrated mechanism. Its structure, actuators, transmissions, and sensors will change. Components may wear, loosen, heat up, become more compliant, develop friction or backlash, lose actuator strength, or experience partial damage. Sensors may drift, become noisy, or stop reporting correctly. A robot that continues controlling itself according to its original factory model may become inaccurate or unsafe even though it remains physically capable of completing its task.

Instead, the intelligent part of the robot should continually estimate what body it currently has. It should notice when the relationship between its commands, internal sensor readings, structural deformation, and resulting motion has changed. It should also represent uncertainty about whether an apparent change came from the structure, an actuator, or the sensors observing them. It could then adjust its internal body model and its behavior so that it preserves as much useful capability as possible rather than treating every deviation from the original model as a terminal failure.

The seed question is:

> **Can a simulated robot use distributed, physically grounded measurements of its own structural state to recognize and compensate for changes in its body or sensors, and does that information provide an adaptive-control advantage beyond a more conventional robot sensor suite?**

I am especially interested in the intersection of three ideas: distributed structural sensing, a body model that can be revised while the robot operates, and adaptation to both mechanical change and sensor degradation. I suspect that related fields may often study these pieces separately—for example, structural health monitoring may concentrate on detecting damage, robot self-modeling may infer changes from motion, tactile sensing may concentrate on external contact, and fault-tolerant control may assume a predefined type of failure. The literature review must determine whether that suspicion is correct. The potentially useful contribution may lie not in inventing any one of these components, but in finding a narrow way to connect them.

Before defining the project’s claim, the agents should use the literature review to develop a strong understanding of current work in robot self-modeling and body-schema learning, adaptive and fault-tolerant control, online system identification, structural health monitoring, flexible- and soft-robot proprioception, artificial skin and whole-body tactile sensing, distributed strain sensing, sensor-fault diagnosis, and embodied or morphological intelligence. The review should identify the strongest existing approaches, what has already been demonstrated in simulation or hardware, which failures and limitations remain, and where these areas already overlap. The project should not merely reproduce a known result in a different simulator. It should identify a defensible direction that pushes beyond current work, even if the advance is deliberately small.

This is intended to be a small exploratory project that could inform a larger research program, not an attempt to solve general humanoid robotics or design a complete artificial nervous system. The agents should sharpen it into the smallest research question that can still reveal whether mechanically meaningful, distributed body sensing is a promising source of information for adaptive robots. A clean negative result would still be valuable: it could show that the proposed structural signals add little beyond conventional sensing, that their usefulness depends on particular kinds of body changes, or that a different representation of bodily state is needed.

The entire project must be completed in simulation. There is no budget for physical components, robot hardware, paid datasets, paid services, or assumed access to laboratory equipment. The agents have the existing Collaboration Station desktop and its gaming-class GPU, and should rely on free, openly available software and resources whose licenses are compatible with the project. The simulated sensors should represent quantities that could plausibly be measured by a future physical system, but this project does not need to produce a manufacturable sensor. Its purpose is to determine whether the information such sensors could provide is valuable enough to justify deeper research.

The larger idea behind the project is that a general robot may need more than an intelligent controller attached to a fixed machine. It may need a body that participates in control, a distributed sensory system that reveals both contact and internal mechanical state, and an intelligence that continually relearns the body it is controlling. This project should investigate one small but concrete step toward that kind of mechanically self-aware robot.
